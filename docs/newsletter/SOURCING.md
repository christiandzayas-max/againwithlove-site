# Sourcing and Citation

The rule is simple. If we cannot trace it to a real, named source, it does not go
in. No guessing, no "caregivers often feel," no invented numbers, no made-up
reader quotes. This is a health-adjacent newsletter for vulnerable families, so
the bar is high.

## Where caregiving content comes from
For the practical sections (Toolkit, One Thing That Helps, For You Too) and for
knowing what caregivers actually struggle with:

- **Caregiver communities.** Reddit (r/dementia, r/AlzheimersGroup,
  r/CaregiverSupport), and the Alzheimer's Association message boards
  (alzconnected.org). Read what people are really asking this week.
- **Reputable caregiving organizations.** Alzheimer's Association (alz.org), the
  National Institute on Aging (nia.nih.gov), Family Caregiver Alliance
  (caregiver.org), AARP caregiving. These are for established caregiving
  guidance, not medical advice.
- **Respected dementia-care educators** (for example, Teepa Snow / Positive
  Approach to Care) for well-known, widely taught techniques.
- **Our own readers.** The Question of the Week turns real replies into real,
  permission-based content and tells us what to write next. Over time this
  becomes our best and most original source.

Note on the sample issue: the "When are you coming home?" example came from the
Again, With Love product brief, which lists the anxious repeated questions the app
was built for. That is fine for a mockup. In production, an experience like that
should be backed by a community source or a real reader reply, not an assumption.

## Where research (On the Horizon) comes from
- **Primary literature.** PubMed (pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov), and preprint servers
  medRxiv and bioRxiv for the earliest work.
- **Trials.** ClinicalTrials.gov for what is actually being tested and at what
  phase.
- **Institutions.** NIH / National Institute on Aging, and the Alzheimer's
  Association, including news from its annual conference (AAIC).
- **Reputable science press** only as a pointer. Always follow it back to the
  primary source and link that.

## How every research item is written
Each item in On the Horizon must include:
1. **A plain-language summary.** What did they actually find, in words a tired
   person understands.
2. **An evidence label.** One of: lab or animal only, early human, clinical trial
   (with phase), peer-reviewed, or preprint (not yet peer-reviewed).
3. **An honest "what this means for you today."** Usually the honest answer is
   "promising, years away, here is what to watch." Say that. Never imply a cure or
   a treatment a reader can act on unless it is genuinely available and you say so
   plainly.
4. **A link to the primary source.**

The Evidence Rater sub-agent assigns the label and blocks anything that cannot be
linked to a primary source.

## Hard lines
- No fabricated statistics, quotes, testimonials, endorsements, or partnerships.
- Reader stories and tips only with explicit permission, and anonymized unless
  the reader wants their first name used.
- No medical, diagnostic, or treatment advice. Point people to their doctor.
- If a claim cannot be verified and linked, cut it. Every time.
